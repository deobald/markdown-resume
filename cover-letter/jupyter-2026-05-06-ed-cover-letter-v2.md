Dear Jupyter Foundation search committee,

I'm excited by the opportunity to help operationalize the Jupyter Foundation's value proposition and grow its membership. Open source ecosystem sustainability has been a passion of mine for decades, and the Jupyter projects sit at one of the most important intersections in modern software: scientific computing, data science, AI/ML, and education. Helping the Foundation translate that significance into tangible value for its members, and clarity for its community, would be an honour.

In 2025, I had the opportunity to serve the GNOME Foundation as Executive Director. My primary focus was balancing the budget, year-on-year, while repairing damaged relationships between the Advisory Board (GNOME's Premier-member equivalent), the board of directors, and the wider community of contributors. The Advisory Board includes Red Hat, Canonical, Google, and SUSE, and a significant amount of my time was spent understanding what each of those organizations actually wanted from their membership, then helping their representatives articulate that value internally. Prior to my time as ED, there was no collaborative space for Advisory Board reps. We kicked off exactly such an online forum at the all-day, in-person Advisory Board meeting in July of 2025, to bring the Advisory Board and the Foundation closer together throughout the year. This kind of work (onboarding members properly, mapping Foundation activities to member goals, building participation mechanisms, and equipping reps to defend their budget internally) is exactly the kind of work the Jupyter ED role centres on, and it is the part of the GNOME ED role I enjoyed the most.

Within a month of starting, I also kicked off a project focused on correcting and diversifying revenue streams. This included new grant applications and commercial relationships with organizations like AWS Open Source, but it primarily focused on a pair of websites: https://donate.gnome.org and https://fellowship.gnome.org. The new donation page provided a clear path to growth of recurring revenue and, in its first few months, more than quadrupled the Foundation's recurring revenue. The Fellowship program, which just launched recently, provides a clear justification for individual and corporate donations. More than anything, the GNOME community was demanding clarity and transparency around the Foundation's operations and intentions, and these two programs not only restabilized the Foundation's finances but also rebuilt years of eroded trust. Long-term GNOME contributors like Jakub Steiner [understood and appreciated the significance of these contributions](https://mastodon.social/@jimmac/115133252419668880) on the long arc of GNOME's multi-decade evolution.

Before the GNOME Foundation, I worked on two open source database startups (XTDB and Endatabas), where much of my role was marketing, narrative-building, and sales. Earlier, I founded and ran Nilenso Software, a worker-owned software cooperative in India, where I was on every sales call and personally closed many of our contracts. Across all three roles, my central responsibility was constructing the narrative for investors, customers, and community members, distinguishing the value proposition by industry and by stakeholder. I expect that work maps cleanly onto the Premier vs. General tiering and the Tech / Finance / Academia segmentation the Foundation is working through.

XTDB and Endatabas also put me in close contact with the data science and ML world, though not with Jupyter projects specifically. Both are HTAP databases designed for the intersection of operations and data science, and XTDB additionally supports bitemporal use cases like ML backtesting. XTDB's "learn XTDB" (and now "learn XTQL") tutorials are built on Clerk, a Clojure-based interactive notebook which draws much inspiration from Jupyter's pioneering notebook work. As general-purpose tools, both products solved fundamental problems in finance, healthcare, e-commerce, GIS, and academia, which overlap with industries the Foundation engages today.

I've delivered multiple keynote speeches over my career, and I genuinely enjoy the challenge of getting to the root of an idea with a team, clarifying it, and presenting it to different audiences. I'm happy to travel and represent the Foundation as its public face, including at JupyterCon, PyData, and other ecosystem events. I love building and supporting teams, especially when the work contributes directly to f/oss.

I'm fortunate to know both Yuvi and April, from the Indian f/oss community and the ThoughtWorks alumni network, respectively. Their descriptions of the wider Jupyter projects and communities have always intrigued me. When I applied for the JupyterHub/Book Community Manager role earlier this year, I joined the Jupyter Zulip and read through many GitHub conversations. The amount of positive and productive energy in and around the Jupyter communities is remarkable, and worth protecting.

I should note that I have no prior experience working directly with the Linux Foundation, so the LF liaison and shared-services side of the role would involve some onboarding on my part. I would lean on board members familiar with LF umbrella services to get up to speed. I'm also comfortable with the part-time contractor structure as currently defined, and equally happy to grow into a broader full-time role if and when that's the right call for the Foundation.

This is a critical juncture for the Jupyter projects. Thanks to the past twelve years of my career, I'm well-equipped to strengthen Foundation members' relationships by bringing clarity to the value of their investment, and to keep the Foundation's positive energy aimed at the work that matters most.

I'm always happy to speak to anyone from the Jupyter Foundation board or community about how I've managed organizations in the past, my philosophy on open source sustainability, or any other question you may have.

All the best,
-steven
